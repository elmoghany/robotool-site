Abstract

RoboTool is a large-scale dataset of human tool-use demonstrations captured in a shared multi-camera rig. We densely cover the combinatorial space of tool, action, and object: the same action (e.g., scoop, stir, crush) is performed with many different physical tools, so every trajectory measures how humans flexibly repurpose objects to accomplish a goal. Each trial captures 8 synchronized RGB-D cameras, and a large fraction of the dataset ships with hand-optimized 6DoF object poses, per-frame masks, and hand trajectories — enabling policy learning, affordance discovery, and tool-use generalization studies.

Dataset at a glance

Collection spans one shared scene (cutting-board / tabletop) with 8 calibrated RGB-D cameras. Each trial is one human demonstration of a (tool, action, object) triple with natural variation in grasp, approach, and in-plane pose. Key takeaway: for almost every action we record, multiple tools are demonstrated, yielding rich tool-choice diversity per goal.

Auto-Segmentation & Annotation

We provide a fully automated annotation pipeline that turns raw multi-camera footage into (1) a metric-scale fused point cloud, (2) DINO-based per-frame foreground masks, and (3) ICP-aligned tool poses — all without manual labels.

What is unique vs 2025/2026 research: recent automatic-annotation pipelines (e.g., SAM2-Track, Grounded-SAM2, ICP-on-DINO baselines) target either single-camera tracking or rigid-only tool pose — not both, and not on a calibrated 8-camera rig with depth co-registration. Our pipeline fuses 8 RealSense streams into a metric point cloud, runs DINO-feature clustering for stable foreground masks across all 8 views, then drives ICP on the per-frame masked point cloud to recover 6-DoF tool trajectories — producing geometry-aware annotations that downstream 3DGS/4DGS pipelines consume directly.

3D Gaussian Splatting Novel-View Synthesis

For static scenes (single timestep, 8 calibrated cameras) we reconstruct a per-scene 3D Gaussian field and synthesize novel views around the captured rig. Our top variant v117 achieves MUSIQ 71.85 — the highest no-reference quality on this scene among all 3DGS pipelines we tested.

What is unique vs 2025/2026 research: sparse-view 3DGS papers (DropGaussian, FSGS, CoR-GS, DNGaussian, AnySplat) all aim for a stronger training-time regularizer to combat under-constrained Gaussians. Our finding is complementary — the dominant remaining quality gap is texture, not geometry, and a single forward pass through a generative view-degradation prior (Difix3D, NVIDIA 2024) closes it. We pair an under-fit SparseGS backbone with an under-used post-processing prior, and the composition is greater than its parts.

Core equations

1. Foundation — 3DGS photometric loss (Kerbl et al., 2023)

  L3DGS = (1−λ)·L1(Render(G,c), Ic) + λ·LD-SSIM
Every method below extends this base loss, swaps the initializer for G, or post-processes its rendered output.

2. InstantSplat family — random init, vary K iters and aux losses (Fan et al. 2024)

  Common form: G0 ∼ uniform(scene);   G = argminG L3DGS;   I(c) = Render(G, c)
• v123 (1k) — baseline, K=1000, no regularizers
• v129 — identical loss but K=7000 (7× longer, tests if more iters alone helps)
• v127 — v123 + a photometric-matching loss across cam pairs:
  Lpm(ci, cj) = ||Π(Wi→j(Ii)) − Ij||2   (warp cam i into cam j via depth, compare)
  Lv127 = L3DGS + λpm·Σ(i,j) Lpm(ci, cj),   K=1000

3. Feed-forward initializers — replace random G0 with a learned scene prior

  Common form: G0 = Φ(Icam0..7);   G = argminG L3DGS(G; G0), K iters
• v118 (MASt3R, Leroy et al. 2024) — Φ = MASt3R (pairwise dense matching), K=1000
• v149b (VGGT-X, Liu et al. 2025) — (G0, κ0) = VGGT (joint scene + camera regression in one forward pass), K=30000

4. Sparsity / surface-regularized losses — add geometric priors on top of L3DGS

• v55 (SparseGS, Xiong et al. 2024) — depth, opacity, and warp regularizers:
  Lv55 = L3DGS + λwarp·Lwarp_reg + λP·LPearson_depth + λOE·Lopacity_entropy
• v150 (Sparfels, Lin et al. 2025) — replaces 3D Gaussians with surface-aligned 2D disks:
  Lv150 = L3DGS + λgeom·Lsparfels-geom + λnorm·Lnormal-consistency
  I(c) = Render2DGS(G, c)   (rasterizer specialized for oriented disks)

5. Diffusion fix-up family (post-render) — apply a pretrained image diffusion to the rendered novel-view sequence

  Common form: Î(c) = D(Render(G, c))   where D is a frozen image-restoration diffusion
• Difix1-step (Liang et al. 2024) — single denoising step at t=199, prompt “remove degradation”:
    v143 — D ∘ InstantSplat-1k
    v117 (best, MUSIQ 71.85) — D ∘ SparseGS (combines categories 4 + 5; +27 MUSIQ over SparseGS alone)
• GSFix4-step (Wang et al. 2025) — 4 denoising steps at t∈{249,499,749,999} with classifier-free guidance:
    v142 — D ∘ MASt3R-3DGS (combines categories 3 + 5)

4D Gaussian Splatting Novel-View Synthesis

For dynamic scenes (10 s · 30 fps · 8 calibrated cameras) we reconstruct a time-aware Gaussian field and synthesize a novel-view trajectory of equal length. Our top variant v383 reaches MUSIQ 72.03 — the first 4DGS pipeline on this scene to exceed the strongest 3DGS-only result (71.85).

What is unique vs 2025/2026 research: recent 4DGS work (4DGaussians/HexPlane CVPR 2024, Spacetime Gaussians CVPR 2024, MEGA ICCV 2025, Faster-GS CVPR 2026) focuses on architectural changes — learned deformation fields, per-Gaussian polynomial trajectories, entropy-constrained compression, accelerated optimization. None target a key empirical observation: per-frame InstantSplat produces images whose artifact distribution exactly matches what generative view-fixers were trained to remove. We show that a per-frame backbone + an ordered diffusion stack (Difix3D→GSFix3D) at 1080p is +1.46 MUSIQ better than the reverse order, and the full pipeline beats every 4DGS baseline by 8–15 MUSIQ on the same scene.

Core equations

1. Per-frame backbone — InstantSplat applied independently to each timestep (no temporal coupling)

  {Gt} = argminG L3DGS(G; cam0..7 @ t),   t∈{0..T−1}   (T = 300 frames)
Reuses the 3DGS L3DGS loss (category 1 above) per timestep; no deformation field, no polynomial trajectory — just a stack of 300 independent 3DGS scenes.

2. Smooth camera path — slerp rotation + lerp position between training cams

  c(s) walks leg order [0,4,7,5,6,2,3,1] (closed loop), s ∈ [0,1)
  Iraw(s) = Render1080p(Gt(s), c(s))
Time and camera are coupled: as s advances along the cam loop, t(s) advances through the 300 timesteps so the rendered video walks through space and time simultaneously.

3. Per-frame diffusion fix-up — reuse the 3DGS diffusion family (category 5 above) on every rendered 1080p frame, then Lanczos ↓ to 720p

  Common form: Î(s) = Lanczos720p(D(Iraw(s)))   where D varies per variant
• v371 (no fix-up) — Î(s) = Iraw(s)   (raw splat output, MUSIQ 55.44)
• v381 (Difix only) — D = Difix1-step   (MUSIQ 63.48; matches 3DGS v143 — per-frame independence does not hurt Difix)
• v382 (GSFix only) — D = GSFix4-step   (MUSIQ 62.92; lower than v381 — Difix is the higher-leverage fixer for this artifact distribution)
• v384 (reversed stack) — D = Difix1-step ∘ GSFix4-step   (MUSIQ 70.57; Difix re-injects high-frequency artifacts GSFix had just removed)
• v383 (best, MUSIQ 72.03) — D = GSFix4-step ∘ Difix1-step   Difix removes splat seams first, then GSFix smooths the residual texture artifacts. Stack order is not commutative: reversing to v384 drops MUSIQ by 1.46.
